Is Online Gambling Legal in California?

Online casino games: No.

Online poker: No.

The Failed Legalization Attempts

Proposition 26 and 27 (November 2022)

California voters faced two competing sports betting measures on the 2022 ballot:

  • **Prop 26** (backed by tribal casinos): Would have allowed in-person sports betting only at tribal casinos and the state's four horse racing tracks. No online betting.
  • **Prop 27** (backed by DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, and other online operators): Would have legalized statewide online sports betting. Lost with 83% voting NO.

    • Prop 27's landslide defeat — despite $200+ million in campaign spending by the online operators — sent a clear signal that Californians aren't ready for fully open online sports betting.

    What's happening in 2026:

    Legislative efforts are ongoing. The tribal casino interests and online operators are trying to find a compromise framework. Most analysts expect a new ballot measure in 2026 or 2028 — but nothing is certain.

    California Gambling Taxes

    California has a 13.3% top marginal income tax rate — the highest in the nation. Gambling winnings are ordinary income and subject to:

  • **Federal:** Standard rates (10-37% depending on total income)
  • **California state:** Up to 13.3%

    • **No deduction for losses** on California state taxes (unlike federal). California is one of the few states that doesn't allow gambling loss deductions on state returns.

    **Practical advice:** Keep records of all wins and losses. If you play regularly, consider speaking with a tax professional — the combined federal + California tax on large winnings can be significant.

    When Will California Legalize Sports Betting?

    Every major sports league, every major operator, and the governor's office have expressed support. The holdout is the tribal casino industry's insistence on maintaining exclusivity over gambling in California.

    The most likely path to legalization requires:

    1. A compromise between tribal casinos and online operators

    2. A ballot measure (California cannot legalize gambling by legislative action alone — it requires voter approval)

    3. Winning that ballot measure with 50%+ of the vote

    **Timeline estimate:** Most industry analysts expect another ballot measure in 2026. Whether it passes depends on whether the tribal/operator coalition can unify behind one proposal instead of competing ones.

    When California legalizes, it will add roughly $3-4 billion in annual gross gaming revenue to the US market — more than any other single state launch in history.
